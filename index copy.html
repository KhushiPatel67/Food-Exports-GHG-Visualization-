Visualization Overview

The above visualization is a fully interactive map of the United States, highlighting agricultural greenhouse gas emissions and food export data from 2011 to 2022. Our team’s goal in designing and creating this map is to explore the connections between economic activity and environmental impact, focusing on emissions from agricultural activities like crop farming and livestock production.

Thanks to the multi-layer complexity of this map, there are many noteworthy patterns to point out. For instance, despite noticeable fluctuations across all states, the Midwest, Texas, and California consistently produce the most metric tons of CO₂ equivalent greenhouse gases (GHGs) over the 12-year time span. This trend is driven by intensive agricultural practices, including livestock production and large-scale crop farming, which contribute to methane and nitrous oxide emissions.

In addition, these high-emission states derive significant portions of their export income from commodities like grains, oilseeds, and livestock products. For example, Iowa's economy is heavily influenced by grain oilseed production, while Texas benefits greatly from cattle and cotton. In contrast, lower-emission states such as Vermont, Rhode Island, and other parts of the Northeast primarily export non-commodity products like value-added agricultural goods, which tend to generate fewer emissions due to less industrialized farming methods.
